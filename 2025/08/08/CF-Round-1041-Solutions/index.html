2127B - Hamiiid, Haaamid… Hamid?

Idea: _R00T and Error_Yuan
Preparation: _R00T and Hamed_Ghaffari

Hints

Hint 1

The answer to the problem only depends on xxx, nnn, the index of the wall Hamid would hit if he moves to the left, and the index of the wall he would hit if he moves to the right.

Hint 2

If we denote the two indices defined in Hint 1 as LLL and RRR, then in each of Hamid’s turns, he can move in a way that ensures by the next time, either LLL has decreased by at least one, or RRR has increased by at least one, regardless of Mani’s moves. So, if it’s Hamid’s turn, what is the maximum possible answer in terms of LLL, RRR, and nnn?

Answer

The answer is at most min⁡(L+1,n−R+2)\min(L + 1, n - R + 2)min(L+1,n−R+2).

Solution

Suppose it’s Hamid’s turn. If there is no wall to his right or to his left, he can escape the grid in one move. Otherwise, define:

  • LLL as the index of the wall Hamid would reach if he moves to the left.
  • RRR as the index of the wall he would reach if he moves to the right.

Hamid can move to the left and decrease LLL by at least one. (Since LLL is always to the left of Hamid’s current position, when Hamid moves left, he reaches and destroys the wall at position L. As a result, on his next turn, the new L will be at least one position further to the left. meaning LLL decreases by at least one, regardless of Mani’s actions.) Similarly, Hamid can increase RRR by at least 111 by moving to the right and destroying the wall there.

So, since Hamid wants to escape in the minimum number of moves, he can escape the grid in at most min⁡(L+1,n−R+2)\min(L + 1, n - R + 2)min(L+1,n−R+2) moves.

On the other hand, with each of Hamid’s moves, Mani can place a wall in such a way that LLL decreases by at most one or R increases by at most one.

So the minimum number of moves Hamid needs is also min⁡(L+1,n−R+2)\min(L + 1, n - R + 2)min(L+1,n−R+2).

Therefore, assuming Hamid starts first, the answer is min⁡(L+1,n−R+2)\min(L + 1, n - R + 2)min(L+1,n−R+2).

Now consider Mani’s first move. Since Mani wants to maximize the number of days Hamid needs. Moreover, with each of his moves, at least one of the LLL or RRR remains unchanged. So, on his first move, he can:

  • maximize L+1L + 1L+1 by setting L=x−1L = x - 1L=x−1, or
  • maximize n−R+2n - R + 2n−R+2 by minimizing RRR, setting it to x+1x + 1x+1.

So the answer becomes either min⁡((x−1)+1,n−R+2)\min((x-1) + 1, n - R + 2)min((x−1)+1,n−R+2) or min⁡(L+1,n−(x+1)+1)\min(L + 1, n - (x+1) + 1)min(L+1,n−(x+1)+1).

Because Mani wants the number of days to be as large as possible, the final answer is: max⁡(min⁡(x,n−R+2),min⁡(L+1,n−x+1))\max(\min(x, n - R + 2), \min(L + 1, n - x + 1))max(min(x,n−R+2),min(L+1,n−x+1))

We can easily find L and R with a time complexity of O(n)\mathcal{O}(n)O(n). Therefore, the problem is solved in O(n)\mathcal{O}(n)O(n) time complexity.

Hints

Hint 1

We can see that permuting arrays aaa and bbb in the same way or changing aia_iai​ with bib_ibi​ for a specific index iii doesn’t change the answer. WLOG assume ai≤bia_i \leq b_iai​≤bi​ is always true.

  2. ai:=x1,bi:=x3,aj:=x2,bj:=x4→ value=x3−x1+x4−x2a_i := x_1, b_i := x_3, a_j := x_2, b_j := x_4 \rightarrow \space value = x_3 - x_1 + x_4 - x_2ai​:=x1​,bi​:=x3​,aj​:=x2​,bj​:=x4​→ value=x3​−x1​+x4​−x2​
  3. ai:=x1,bi:=x4,aj:=x2,bj:=x3→ value=x4−x1+x3−x2a_i := x_1, b_i := x_4, a_j := x_2, b_j := x_3 \rightarrow \space value = x_4 - x_1 + x_3 - x_2ai​:=x1​,bi​:=x4​,aj​:=x2​,bj​:=x3​→ value=x4​−x1​+x3​−x2​

Other cases are similar (based on Hint 111). We observe that the second and third options yield the same valuevaluevalue, while the first option is exactly 2×(x3−x2)2 \times (x_3 - x_2)2×(x3​−x2​) less than the other two. There are now two possible cases:

Case 1: Ali can choose two indices iii and jjj such that the ordering of [ai,bi,aj,bj][a_i, b_i, a_j, b_j][ai​,bi​,aj​,bj​] already matches one of the second or third options. In this case, Bahamin cannot increase the value when he is initially given indices iii and jjj. Since Ali also cannot reduce the valuevaluevalue, his optimal strategy is to always give Bahamin such indices so that the final valuevaluevalue remains equal to the initial valuevaluevalue.

Case 2: Ali cannot find any two indices iii and jjj that match the second or third option. This implies that for every pair of indices i,ji, ji,j, either b_i &lt; a_j or b_j &lt; a_i. We claim the answer is \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n} (b_i - a_i) + \min\limits_{1 \leq i, j \leq n, \space b_i &lt; a_j} (2 \times (a_j - b_i)).

A strategy for Ali is to always select the two indices i,ji, ji,j such that b_i &lt; a_j and the difference aj−bia_j - b_iaj​−bi​ is minimized. Bahamin can increase the valuevaluevalue only once — when he changes the configuration of indices i,ji, ji,j from the first option to either the second or third option. We know this increases the initial valuevaluevalue by exactly 2×(aj−bi)2 \times (a_j - b_i)2×(aj​−bi​). Also, it’s impossible for Ali to force any valuevaluevalue lower than this. Since regardless of Ali’s selection, Bahamin can always change it to the second or third option, and by definition, any choice by Ali gives Bahamin a chance to increase valuevaluevalue by at least our claimed amount. Also Bahamin should skip his turn (i.e., make no changes) on all subsequent turns after the first.

Now to calculate the answer so that it fits the time limit, let’s first represent our array as a set of nnn segments of the form [ai,bi][a_i, b_i][ai​,bi​] on the number line. An interesting property of this representation is that the first option now corresponds to two completely separate intervals, while the second and third options correspond to two intersecting intervals.

Now finding the answer becomes relatively easy. Sort the intervals by their left endpoint. If there are two neighboring intervals that intersect, then the input satisfies the Case 1 condition, and so the answer is the initial valuevaluevalue. Otherwise, we observe that all pairs of intervals are non-intersecting, and thus the input satisfies the Case 2 condition. The answer, based on Case 2 and our earlier definitions, is 2×(distance between closest two segments)+(initial value)2 \times (\text{distance between closest two segments}) + (\text{initial value})2×(distance between closest two segments)+(initial value).

The two closest intervals must also be adjacent after sorting by their left endpoint. So, by computing the minimum distance between each pair of neighboring intervals, we obtain a solution in O(nlog⁡n)\mathcal{O}(n \log n)O(nlogn).

Since LLL must have another neighbor besides vvv, that neighbor cannot be on the southern side, so it must be on the northern side. However, since vvv is the leftmost node, this neighbor must be to the right of vvv, and the edge between LLL and this neighbor would cross the edge vvv—LLL. This contradiction shows that LLL cannot have another neighbor besides vvv, which contradicts our assumption that the graph is a cycle.

Therefore, it is impossible to arrange the vertices of this tree in a valid Destinyland layout.

From Lemma 1 and Lemma 2, we conclude that:

  • GGG must be a tree.
  • No vertex in GGG can be connected to three or more non-leaf neighbors.

In this case, the original graph was just a single edge. So the answer is simply 222.

2127E - Ancient Tree

Idea: Bahamin
Preparation: Bahamin and Hamed_Ghaffari

Hints

Hint 1

There are some vertices that will always be cutie, no matter how the other vertices are colored. What are these vertices?

Hint 2

We can show that there exists a coloring where only the vertices mentioned in Hint 1 become cutie. So, the final answer is the sum of the weights of the vertices mentioned in Hint 1.

Solution

Read the hints.

We will use a very useful and interesting technique called the Virtual Tree (or Auxiliary Tree). You can learn more here: Virtual Tree. There are other solutions to this problem, but they use a similar core idea.

The virtual tree of a set of vertices like SSS is defined as this set:

A=S∪{lca⁡(u,v)∣u,v∈S}A = S \cup \{\operatorname{lca}(u, v) \mid u, v \in S \}A=S∪{lca(u,v)∣u,v∈S}

Now let’s build this virtual tree for the vertices of each color separately. After this, all vertices fall into three categories:

  1. Vertices that are in no virtual tree.
  2. Vertices that are in exactly one virtual tree.
  3. Vertices that appear in more than one virtual tree.

Every vertex like uuu of the third type will definitely be cutie, because no matter what color it is assigned, at least one of the colors of the virtual trees it belongs to will be different from uuu's color.

So the answer is at least the sum of the weights of all type-3 vertices.

To construct an example with exactly this answer, run a DFSDFSDFS algorithm. Every time you enter a vertex uuu, consider the following cases:

  • It’s type 2. In that case, color it with the color of the virtual tree it belongs to.
  • It’s type 3. In that case, color it with any color from its subtree (such a color exists by definition). This vertex is cutie anyway.
  • It’s type 1 and there is some colored vertex in its subtree. Then color this vertex with that color.
  • It’s type 1 and its entire subtree is uncolored. In that case, color this vertex with its parent’s color.

To prove this coloring is valid, observe that coloring a vertex uuu with a color from its subtree does not make any new vertices cutie (because any such vertex would already have become cutie due to the same color in uuu's subtree). Also, no vertex strictly inside uuu's subtree changes status. And uuu itself doesn’t increase the cost based on how we chose its color.

Now, for the case where we color uuu with its parent’s color: if any new cutie vertex appears, it must be the parent. But since both have the same color, there’s no additional cost.

Also, in this constructed example, we never introduce any new colors. So all colors stay within the range [1,k][1, k][1,k].

Therefore, the final answer is always the sum of the weights of type-3 vertices—those that appear in more than one virtual tree. These virtual trees can be constructed in O(nlog⁡n)\mathcal{O}(n \log n)O(nlogn).

Hint 2.1

When we ask a permutation, what does asking the reverse of the permutation help us with?

Hint 2.2

If we ask the reverse of the permutation except the index kkk, how many times is each edge of the permutation graph counted in total?

Hint 3

How to choose kkk?

Solution

Let xxx be a number that we have not yet fixed, and let k=x+1k = x + 1k=x+1.

This partitions the array into three segments:

$ \begin{aligned} A &= [1, x] \ B &= [x + 2, n - x] \ C &= [n - x + 1, n] \end{aligned}$

This tripartition helps in controlling the query behavior.

Queries:

Say we want to find out who maps to vertex iii, i.e., find jjj such that pj=ip_j = ipj​=i.

Here’s how:

  1. Construct a permutation qqq such that qk=iq_k = iqk​=i
  2. Query this permutation →→→ receive answer aaa
  3. Reverse all elements of qqq except the position kkk
  4. Query the reversed permutation →→→ receive answer bbb

If we consider the permutation graph of p:

  • Any edge not involving iii contributes once to the total count. Why?
  • The edge where pj=ip_j = ipj​=i:
    • Will not be counted if j=qkj = q_kj=qk​. This is not possible because it is guaranteed in the question that pi≠ip_i\neq ipi​​=i.
    • Will be counted in only one of the two queries if j∈q[A∪C]j \in q[A \cup C]j∈q[A∪C].
    • Will be counted neither if j∈q[B]j \in q[B]j∈q[B].
  • The edge where pi=jp_i = jpi​=j will not be counted at all, because iii is at index kkk.

Thus, a+ba + ba+b is one of [n−2,n−1][n - 2, n - 1][n−2,n−1] and by comparing a+ba + ba+b, we can find which segment jjj (the predecessor of iii) lies in.

Repeating the Process:

Once you know the predecessor is in a certain half, you repeat:

  • Repartition the candidate set into two
  • Choose one half to be placed in A∪CA \cup CA∪C, and the other in BBB (If we had enough capacity).
  • Place iii at index kkk again.
  • Do the same query + reverse trick

Choosing k:

If we set x=⌊n+14⌋,k=x+1x = \lfloor \frac{n+1}{4} \rfloor,\quad k = x + 1x=⌊4n+1​⌋,k=x+1, with each iteration (both in the first query and in the rest of the queries) the size of the candidate set shrinks by a factor of 2.

So to find the predecessor of one number iii, we need 2log⁡(n−1)2 \log (n - 1)2log(n−1) queries.

To find the entire permutation of nnn elements 2nlog⁡(n−1)2 n\log (n - 1)2nlog(n−1) queries.

We stay well within the 15⋅n15 \cdot n15⋅n limit.

Analysis (Steps & Hints)

Step 0

Hint 1.1

Step 1.2

Hint 2.2

Step 2.3

Step 3.1

How can we distinguish i→pii\to p_ii→pi​ is in which kind of cycle, length of 222 or length ≥3\ge 3≥3? Try to solve this for a single iii in 2log⁡n2\log n2logn queries. Note that you do not need to determine pip_ipi​, you just need to tell whether i→pii\to p_ii→pi​ is in a cycle of length 222.

Hint 3.2

If i→pii\to p_ii→pi​ is in a cycle of length ≥3\ge 3≥3, we have lll and jjj such that l→i→jl\to i\to jl→i→j and l≠jl\ne jl​=j. Otherwise, l=jl=jl=j.

Hint 3.3

Consider bitmasks.

Hint 3.4

Step 3.5

Consider the following two queries (m&lt;k):

  1. [q1,q2,q3,…,qm,…,qk,…][q_1, q_2, q_3, \ldots, q_m, \ldots, q_k, \ldots][q1​,q2​,q3​,…,qm​,…,qk​,…];
  2. [qm,q1,q2,…,qm−1,…,qk,…][q_m, q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_{m-1}, \ldots, q_k, \ldots][qm​,q1​,q2​,…,qm−1​,…,qk​,…].

Only the contribution of x→qm→yx\to q_m\to yx→qm​→y may change: Let’s say A={q1,q2,…,qm−1}A=\{q_1,q_2,\ldots,q_{m-1}\}A={q1​,q2​,…,qm−1​}, B={qm+1,qm+2,…qn}B=\{q_{m+1}, q_{m+2}, \ldots q_n\}B={qm+1​,qm+2​,…qn​}. If the responses are different, we can next tell whether xix_ixi​ is in AAA by looking at whether the difference is 111 or −1-1−1. However, if the responses are the same, we can’t tell whether they are both in AAA or both in BBB.

An idea to tell whether x=yx=yx=y is to group numbers by their bitmasks. We can use log⁡n\log nlogn times such two queries to ask for each bit (let numbers with 0 on this bit in AAA, those with 1 in BBB). In the end, we will get x⊕yx\oplus yx⊕y. Then we surely get whether x=yx=yx=y.

Hint 3.6

Step 3.5 needs 2log⁡n2\log n2logn queries for a single element. Can you use nlog⁡nn\log nnlogn queries to solve the problem in Step 3.5 for all elements? Note that we are doing cyclic shifts.

Step 3.7

Note that we are doing cyclic shifts. In fact, for each iii, we just need it to appear at q1q_1q1​ and qmq_mqm​ respectively, without changing the set AAA. On average for each element, it is at most 13log⁡n\frac{1}{3}\log n31​logn queries.

Preparation: Hamed_Ghaffari and _R00T

Solution

Each connected component of a candy graph is either a simple path or a simple cycle.

Take a DFS tree, at most 555 back-edges pass through each vertex, then choose a vertex vvv, brute force on the back-edges that pass through vvv and appears in the subgraph with maximum number of edges, then remove vvv and all back-edges that pass through vvv.

Let did_idi​ be the maximum degree that vertex iii can have. Initially di=2d_i=2di​=2 for each vertex. Each back-edge (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) that you select, reduces dxd_xdx​ and dyd_ydy​ by 111. We can solve the sub-problem with the following dp bitmask:

  • Add a vertex to the last path: dpmask,u,v,l1+1→dpmask+2w,u,w,0dp_{mask,u,v,l1}+1 \to dp_{mask+2^w,u,w,0}dpmask,u,v,l1​+1→dpmask+2w,u,w,0​, if www is a vertex outside maskmaskmask and adjacent to vvv, and dv=2,dw≥1d_v = 2, d_w \geq 1dv​=2,dw​≥1.

Also, you have to solve the sub-problem for each component twice, because you can choose a DFS tree edge connected to the vertex that you’ve deleted and it reduces ddd of one vertex in that component.

It can be seen that if NNN be the size of the component with the maximum size, the complexity will be O(2NNm)\mathcal{O}(2^NNm)O(2NNm) for a fixed set of back-edges, and O(252NNm)\mathcal{O}(2^52^NNm)O(252NNm) in total.

If we select vvv as the centroid of the DFS tree NNN will be less than or equal to n/2n/2n/2 so the complexity will be O(252n/2nm)\mathcal{O}(2^5 2^{n/2}nm)O(252n/2nm).

It can be proven that m≤3nm \leq 3nm≤3n in graphs that each vertex belongs to at most 555 simple cycles. So the complexity will be O(252n/2n2)\mathcal{O}(2^52^{n/2}n^2)O(252n/2n2).

Proof

We prove this by induction. suppose that m≤3nm \leq 3nm≤3n for all graphs with n<Nn \lt Nn<N.

Take a DFS tree, we can prove that the degree of a leaf is at most 333, because otherwise at least 333 back-edges would exit that leaf and there would be at least 666 cycles containing that leaf.

So there always exists a vertex with degree ≤3\leq 3≤3, we can remove it, m≤3(N−1)+3=3Nm \leq 3(N-1) + 3 = 3Nm≤3(N−1)+3=3N.
